A6: Talking to the web

For this assignment we were tasked with creating a webpage that interacts with and changes something in the real world and vice versa, doing something in the real world changes something on our webpage.

This is an image of my final circuit built on a breadboard and connected to the Arduino which is connected to my computer. I have two button connected to digital pins 2 and 3, and two LEDs connected to pwd pins 5 and 6. Each button and LED has their own resistor.

Circuit Diagram

Here is the schematic diagram for my circuit. There are two LEDs and two buttons, each in their own branch.

I used a 10k ohm resistor to act as a pull-down resistor on the button branch, as recommended in class. The purpose of a pull-down resistor is to ensure that the input pin reads LOW when the button is not pressed. When the button is not pressed, Pin 2 might pick-up additional noise from the environment causing it to read a non-zero voltage. Adding a resistor that connects Pin 2 to Ground, ensures the pin will read zero. I used a 10k ohm because it limits the amount of current flowing through the circuit while still effectively pulling the pin to ground.

Choosing Resistors

To choose the right resistors for each branch, I started by measuring the voltage drop through each LED. Each LED had a voltage drop between 1.8 and 3.0 volts, so I rounded up to either 2.0 or 3.0 volts for each LED. I knew the arduino provided 5 volts of power and I wanted a current of 20 mA. Then I used Ohm's Law (V = IR) to calculate the resistance needed. The result was a 100 or 150 ohm resistor for each branch. To ensure that my the current would be 20mA or lower, I rounded up, using a 220 ohm resistor for each LED branch.
